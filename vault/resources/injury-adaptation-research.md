# Injury-Adaptation Research — Jordan Shallow / Pre-Script + Clinical Sources
*Compiled July 2026 from Shallow's own Pre-Script material and corroborating clinical/rehab sources. Purpose: source material for authoring the injury/rehab adaptation ruleset that VitalEdge's `COACHING_KNOWLEDGE` currently lacks (per the Manus injury trace — injury handling is presently LLM-discretion off free-text notes, no code, no structural rules).*

> **Scope & safety framing.** This is research to inform program *generation* guardrails, not medical advice. The output ruleset should: (a) bias exercise selection away from provocative positions, (b) prefer graded/tolerable variations, (c) flag for coach review, and (d) never diagnose. Anything acute, severe, or neurological (numbness, shooting/radiating pain, post-surgical) → the rule is "flag for coach + recommend professional assessment," not "substitute exercise X." Coach names (Shallow etc.) stay out of client-facing output.

## PART 1 — Jordan Shallow / Pre-Script core framework
*This is the organising philosophy. The per-region protocols in Part 2 are the concrete application.*

**Who/what.** Dr. Jordan Shallow (DC, "The Muscle Doc"), founder of Pre-Script® with Dr. Jordan Jiunta. Chiropractor + strength coach + competitive powerlifter. Programs used in 15+ countries. Co-host RX'd Radio. The methodology is applied biomechanics for lifters — making training productive *and* joint-safe, not clinic-style corrective work divorced from the barbell.

**The Mobility → Stability → Strength® model (in that order, every pattern).**
The central sequence and the backbone of any injury rule:
1. **Mobility** — free up the full range of motion of the joint first.
2. **Stability** — light/bodyweight drills to gain *control* of that range (load it neurologically before mechanically).
3. **Strength** — only once the range is controlled, add meaningful load.
The failure mode Shallow names: **"loading unstable muscles is what causes injuries."** You must load a muscle both anatomically and neurologically. Skipping straight to strength on an uncontrolled range is the injury mechanism.

**Injury *risk management*, not injury *prevention*.** Shallow is explicit that you can't "prevent" injury — you manage risk via load management. Programming implication: the lever is *load and exposure*, not exercise bans. An aggravated joint gets its load and provocative-range exposure reduced, then re-introduced gradually — not the movement deleted forever.

**Function over sensation (the selection filter).** Repeated Pre-Script theme: *"sensation does not equal stimulation."* Exercises are wrongly chosen for how they *feel* on the target muscle. Select for the muscle's actual biomechanical *function* and where it's actually loaded — not the pump. (His "sumo deadlift is not a glute exercise" and "back squat bar position is not a universal truth" pieces are both this principle: individual anatomy dictates selection, there are no universally-mandatory exercises.)

**Graded exposure to compromised ranges under load.** From his shoulder-rotation work: warm-ups only fix restricted range *temporarily*; **lasting change needs graded exposure to the compromised range under meaningful load.** So the rehab-aware rule isn't "avoid the range" forever — it's "reduce load, keep controlled exposure, progressively reload the range."

**Training outside active ROM is not automatically unsafe.** Shallow pushes back on the clinical default of "only train within active range for safety" — calls it "not remotely black-and-white." Implication for rules: don't hard-ban end-range or lengthened-position work on an injured joint by default; it can be trained with control and appropriate load. This is a nuance — the ruleset should reduce/adapt, not blanket-forbid.

**Neutral scapular plane = mechanical advantage.** For shoulder/arm work, operating in the neutral scapular plane gives the musculature a natural mechanical advantage to do more work — relevant to why neutral-grip and scapular-plane pressing are shoulder-friendlier.

**Key Pre-Script exercise concepts worth encoding as "preferred rehab-aware tools":**
- **KB windmill** — exposes/trains thoracic rotation, shoulder ER/IR, scapular upward/downward rotation, ribcage function in one movement. A go-to for shoulder + t-spine function.
- **High cable bicep curl** — develops shoulder function *while* giving bicep hypertrophy stimulus (dual-purpose corrective).
- **T-spine extension/flexion capacity** — named a "non-negotiable" precursor to shoulder mobility/function; the shoulder complex can't access end ranges without it.
- **Upright row** — Shallow explicitly *defends* it against the impingement panic (it's "collateral damage" of impingement fear). Nuance for rules: don't auto-ban it; it's provocative for *some* shoulders, not inherently dangerous.

**Sources (Part 1):** blog.pre-script.com (Shallow's current article series — "Applying Applied Biomechanics") · pre-script.com (PSL1 16-week course) · Barbell Shrugged ep. 296 · Muscle Maven Radio ep. 12 · RX'd Radio · @the_muscle_doc / @pre_script.

## PART 2 — Per-region adaptation protocols
*Structure for each region: provocative patterns to reduce/avoid · preferred substitutions · programming levers · escalation flag. Drawn from clinical/rehab sources and consistent with Part 1's load-management framing.*

### 2A. Shoulder (rotator-cuff-related pain / impingement / RCRSP)
**Provocative — reduce load / range first:** heavy flat barbell bench at full ROM; wide-grip pressing (arms flared to 90° abduction — the main driver of impingement-type pain); behind-the-neck pressing/pulldowns; deep dips; full-ROM overhead barbell press when shoulder flexion range is limited or painful; upright row *for sensitive shoulders* (individual — not a blanket ban, per Shallow).
**Preferred substitutions (shoulder-friendlier):**
- Press: **neutral-grip DB press, DB floor press** (reduced ROM, more tolerable), **landmine press** (diagonal plane, no overhead lockout, scalable range), **machine press**, **incline press** as an overhead entry point.
- Cue: press with a **slight elbow tuck (~45° torso-arm angle, not 90°)** to reduce anterior shoulder load; scapular retraction+depression without a powerlifting back-arch.
- Overhead progression (only once pain-free with the above + full ROM): bottoms-up KB press → neutral/hammer-grip DB press → traditional DB press → barbell.
- Foundational/scapular + cuff: rows, band pull-aparts, prone Y/T raises, external rotations, serratus work (scap punch), scapular push-ups.
**Programming levers:** limit horizontal shoulder abduction; keep total chest volume ~12–18 sets/wk; reduce load before range; slow controlled tempo; progress range/load only when the current stage is pain-free (or pain stable, not worsening, rep-to-rep).
**Escalation flag:** night pain, significant weakness, pain not improving with load reduction, any traumatic mechanism → coach review + professional assessment.

### 2B. Lower back (lumbar — general mechanical / disc-history)
**Provocative — reduce load / range first:** heavy conventional deadlifts to failure; heavy loaded spinal flexion under fatigue; touch-and-go deadlift cycling where the brace is lost; heavy good-mornings; loaded end-range lumbar flexion or hyperextension; high-rep barbell work late in a fatigued session (many flare-ups happen late, not on first sets, as trunk-control endurance fades).
**Preferred substitutions / modifications:**
- Hinge with less spinal shear/load: trap-bar deadlift, elevated/block pulls (reduced range), RDL to controlled range, hip thrust / glute bridge (posterior chain, spine-sparing), 45° back extension, chest-supported options.
- Squat pattern: box squat, safety-bar or front-loaded (more upright torso), machine/hack, leg press (spine-unloaded quad work).
- Build trunk endurance, not just single-rep bracing: carries, planks/anti-rotation (Pallof), McGill-style stability.
**Programming levers:** **bracing** (big breath into belly, brace as if to take a punch, hold intra-abdominal pressure through the rep; think "soda can not water bottle"); keep the bar close; avoid the "stripper pull" (hips rising faster than chest); manage load progression so it doesn't outpace tissue adaptation; note that mobility limits at hips/ankles shift stress to the spine — address those. Deadlift training *can* be rehabilitative when coached and loaded appropriately (evidence-supported) — so the rule is reduce+coach, not eliminate.
**Escalation flag:** radiating/shooting leg pain, numbness/tingling, weakness, bladder/bowel changes, traumatic onset → stop, coach review + urgent professional assessment (do not program around these).

### 2C. Knee (patellar / quadriceps tendinopathy)
**Provocative — reduce load / range / rate first:** deep-ROM heavy squats (patellar/quad tendon load rises with depth and with knee travelling forward over toes; pain often only at the very bottom); fast/high-rate loading (tendons are sensitive to *rate* of loading more than magnitude) — plyometrics, jump work, explosive/rebound reps; sudden load increases.
**Preferred substitutions / staged loading (heavy-slow-resistance + isometrics lineage):**
- **Isometrics for pain relief + capacity:** Spanish squat holds (70–90° knee flexion, strap-anchored) or isometric leg-extension holds — e.g. 5 × 45s at ~70% effort, ~2 min rest; produces analgesia for hours and can be used pre-session or as standalone 1–3×/day.
- **Isotonic once tolerable:** high box squats, wall sits (shallower knee angle = less tendon load, deepen as tolerance builds), leg press through a pain-free range, heavy-slow-resistance squats/leg extensions (slow tempo), eccentric decline-board single-leg squats.
- Keep knee tracking over toes, avoid valgus collapse.
**Programming levers:** the **pain-monitoring model** — training into pain is acceptable if it stays ≤ ~3–5/10, is stable rep-to-rep, and (critically) **next-morning symptoms haven't worsened** (next-day response is the real load gauge). Control depth and rate before load. Reduce volume/intensity if 24h symptoms spike.
**Escalation flag:** knee giving way, locking, significant swelling, inability to bear weight, traumatic twist → coach review + assessment.

### 2D. Elbow (lateral epicondylalgia / "weightlifter's" / triceps tendinopathy)
**Provocative — reduce total stress first:** high-volume power-grip work (deadlifts, pull-ups, lat pulldowns, heavy loaded carries); heavy pressing near failure; training near 1RM; recent spikes in intensity/volume/frequency of gripping work. Note tendon load includes *non-gym* stress (typing, daily gripping) — total load matters.
**Preferred substitutions / modifications:**
- Reduce grip demand: **lifting straps** to offload the tendon on pulls/carries; neutral-grip variations; machines/cables that reduce forearm loading; reduce pressing intensity/failure proximity.
- Loading rehab: **isometric wrist flexion/extension holds** (30–45s, low load); progressive wrist extensor/flexor loading; forearm work through pain-free range; BFR wrist-extensor training is an evidence-supported option.
**Programming levers:** overuse means you're already above tendon capacity — the fix is *lowering* aggravating training variables (intensity/volume/failure) so rehab exposure can work, not adding more hard sets. Patience over chasing tolerance limits each session.
**Escalation flag:** significant loss of grip strength, locking, traumatic onset, no improvement over weeks of load management → coach review + assessment.

### 2E. Hip (tendinopathy / bursitis / general)
**Provocative — reduce load first:** repetitive high-volume hip flexion/loaded ranges that aggravate; sudden load spikes; heavy work into painful end-range hip flexion.
**Preferred substitutions / modifications:** bias posterior-chain work that offloads the spine and the irritated tissue; gluteal-tendinopathy loading via **hip abduction** isometrics/isotonics; modify volume/impact temporarily (walk vs jog, shorter ranges) during flare; single-leg stability as a monitoring/loading tool.
**Programming levers:** activity modification during flares (reduce impact/volume, not necessarily stop); progressive loading of the involved tendon; monitor next-day response.
**Escalation flag:** deep hip-joint pain, catching/clicking with pain, significant ROM loss, traumatic onset → coach review + assessment.

## PART 3 — Universal rules (apply across all regions)
These generalise the per-region material into the logic the ruleset should encode:

1. **Reduce, don't delete.** Default action on a flagged injury is reduce load / reduce provocative range / reduce rate — not permanently ban the movement. (Shallow's risk-management framing + evidence that loaded training aids most tendinopathies/back pain.)
2. **Isometrics first for irritable tendons** (knee, elbow, shoulder cuff): they reduce pain and build capacity when isotonic work is too provocative. Standard-ish dose: ~5 × 30–45s holds, moderate effort, before or instead of provocative loading.
3. **The next-day test is the real load gauge.** In-session tolerable pain (≤3–5/10, stable rep-to-rep) is acceptable *if* next-morning symptoms haven't worsened. Worsening 24h response = reduce volume/intensity. This is the single most encodable "did we load right?" rule.
4. **Mobility → Stability → Strength ordering** for the affected pattern: restore/keep range, control it lightly, then reload.
5. **Total load, not just gym load** (esp. tendons): account for daily-life stress; the lever is often reducing gym stress so rehab work can take effect.
6. **Select for function, not sensation**; there are no mandatory exercises — always a substitution exists.
7. **Hard escalation triggers → flag for coach + recommend professional assessment, do NOT auto-program:** radiating/shooting pain, numbness/tingling, weakness, giving way/locking, significant swelling, night pain, traumatic mechanism, post-surgical, or anything not improving with load reduction.

## PART 4 — How this maps to VitalEdge (implementation notes, for the ruleset build)

The gap Manus found: injury text sits in free-text `notes`, reaches only the *user* prompt, and there is **no rule in `COACHING_KNOWLEDGE`** telling the LLM how to adapt. This research is the raw material to close that. Suggested encoding:

- **A structured injury block in `COACHING_KNOWLEDGE`** (system prompt) keyed by region (shoulder / lower back / knee / elbow / hip / other), each with: provocative-pattern list, preferred-substitution list, programming levers, and the escalation triggers. Parts 2–3 above are already in that shape.
- **A region parser** on `notes` (or, better, a future structured injury field in the wizard) to detect region + severity keywords and inject only the relevant region block — same conditional pattern as the program-library integration, so the prompt doesn't carry all regions every time.
- **Escalation short-circuit:** if severity/neuro keywords are present (shooting, numbness, gave way, post-op, etc.), the generator should down-scope to a conservative program + a prominent "flag for coach review / recommend assessment" note, rather than confidently programming around a red-flag injury.
- **Ties to existing methodology:** `rehab_biomechanics` (the Shallow overlay in `coach-methodologies-research.md`) is the *philosophy*; this file is the *actionable ruleset* that philosophy implies. The Mobility→Stability→Strength ordering, function-over-sensation selection, and load-management framing are the connective tissue.
- **Boundary check:** all of the above is exercise-selection / range / rate / volume guidance — it stays inside VitalEdge's logging-only boundary (no load computation, no progression math).

**This file is research input, not the final ruleset.** Next step (separate session): turn Parts 2–3 into the exact `COACHING_KNOWLEDGE` injury block + the region-detection logic, then test that generated programs actually respect it — the same author-then-verify discipline as the library integration.
